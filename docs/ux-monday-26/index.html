Is the design process dead?

I wrote about this in 2024: "Is Coding Dead?"

  • AI amplifies existing skills
    • does not replace them
  • Human oversight stays essential throughout
  • Prompt quality is itself a craft skill
  • Best practices are emerging, but the process stays but (constantly) changes
Rhetorical question - but grounded in something real. The article explored AI-assisted prototyping: 30-minute card-sorting prototype, no developer, just Claude. Key finding: AI amplifies existing skills rather than replacing them. Human oversight stays essential. Prompt quality matters. NNg says design process isn't dead - and I agree. But "not dead" and "unchanged" are very different things.

What has changed

  • Writing standard software is a solved problem
    • BAU dashboards, reports, forms: done
  • Execution cost of coding is collapsing
    • "everyone" can code now
  • SaaS is under pressure
"Garbage in, garbage out" - if your knowledge base is a mess, AI won't save you. The inconsistency you've been tolerating becomes a structural problem. The sooner you curate for machines, the sooner new types of value for humans can emerge.

What won't change soon

  • Doing the right thing
  • Human emotions, aspirations, frustrations
  • The value of face time
  • Being humane to each other
"If it becomes easier to make more things, it also becomes easier to make the wrong things."
— Karri Saarinen
This is where design lives. Not in the execution layer - in the judgment layer. That's not going away.

Designers are masters of process

  • Enormous opportunity to shape how experiences are delivered
  • Own the full journey - from user need to shipped experience
  • Orchestrate workflows, don't just execute tasks
  • Don't be afraid to take the lead here
The people who understand the full journey - discovery to delivery - are exactly what's needed to orchestrate these new workflows. That's you. If a tool can spin up a decent brand manual in an hour, what's the value proposition for commodity design work? The opportunity is on the other end: designers are masters of process. That's enormously valuable right now. Don't run from the change - shape it.

Part 1

Discovery

Take a breath. Shift gears. "Let me show you how my research practice has changed."

Bydlo

  • Side-project with my friend Franta
  • Freelance marketplace for architects and interior designers
  • One-off consultations · smaller contracts · new leads
  • How does research on something like this actually start?
My partner is an architect. I'm around a lot of people from that world. A friend noticed a gap - how do architects find smaller, flexible gigs? Walk the audience through the full research workflow step by step.

Step 1: The brain dump

  • Walk outside
  • Think aloud - pile thoughts on top of each other
  • Don't structure yet. Let it be messy.
    • or differently - LLM can untangle for you
  • Brad Frost calls this "mouth coding"
    • bradfrost.com/blog/post/mouth-coding/
I recorded myself thinking through the scope of this project on a walk - dog interruptions, unfinished sentences, mid-thought redirects. The AI listened, didn't lose patience, captured what mattered. What you get from this: project scope, initial hunches, open questions, what you already know vs. what you need to find out. And yes - this entire presentation started exactly this way. Brad Frost recently coined "mouth coding" for the same idea - talking interfaces into existence through live conversation with AI. I call it the brain dump method. Same instinct: speak first, structure later.

Step 2: Connect your second brain

  • MCP - a universal tool connector
    • Lets your agent talk to external tools and data sources
    • Think of it as a USB port to other tools
  • Connect once → "80/20 head start" on every project
MCP stands for Model Context Protocol - Anthropic open-sourced it, now the whole industry uses it. The simplest way to explain it: it's a universal tool connector. Like a USB port - one standard, anything can plug in. Your LLM stays the same; you just keep adding tools. My notes in Obsidian are connected via MCP. Before a single interview, the AI can already help draft a research objective, spot gaps in the brief, sketch stakeholder mapping, pitch recruiting criteria - because my accumulated knowledge from past projects is right there as active input, not a passive archive. Book highlights, method notes, past reflections, all of it. This is what I mean by curating for machines: the sooner your notes are structured for it, the more useful they become at project kickoff.

This is context engineering

  • MCP connection = directing the agent toward your accumulated knowledge
  • Not the internet's average. Yours.
  • Output quality reflects your craft - your biases, your blind spots
  • Even a messy vault beats none - brief notes over perfect ones
People talk a lot about "context engineering" - this is what it looks like in practice. When you connect to your second brain, you're not just giving the AI more data. You're biasing it toward your way of thinking, your prior work, your craft. The output reflects that. My vault is chaotic. I don't maintain it religiously. It's tilted - heavy in some areas, almost empty in others. It still works. The point isn't a perfect knowledge base - it's directionality. Honest constraint: where my notes are thin (strategy, business framing), the AI reflects that gap. Knowing it lets you work with it intentionally rather than being surprised by it.

Pick what fits your setup

  • Notion · Google Drive · Confluence · Obsidian · a shared folder
  • Whatever you have, are allowed to use, or actually like to use
  • I use Obsidian
    • plain text, vendor-agnostic, backlinks help connect the dots
  • The tool choice is secondary.
The tool is secondary. What matters is that your context is somewhere the agent can reach it. Why I use Obsidian: it's plain text - vendor-agnostic, works with any tool now or five years from now. Simple frontmatter metadata, easy to maintain, easy for agents to parse. And linking - I think this one actually matters, though I'm not certain. When you ask the agent to look up "how to write a good research plan," it doesn't just find that note. It follows the links: research plan - research goals - research questions - interview groups - qualitative research - case studies. It traverses the conceptual graph you've already built, rather than treating everything as a flat pile of documents.

Now you have a plan

  • Research objective
  • Interview script - with "pause here, go deeper" instructions baked in
  • Recruitment criteria
  • Stakeholder map
  • All in markdown. In a shared repo.
Anyone on the team can read it. Any AI tool can query it. No tool license required. This is also the foundation of the client-empowerment story coming up - high-quality artifacts let non-researchers run sessions without you. The script quality is what made that work.
"Talk me through the last time X happened..."
This part doesn't change. You still need to sit with people and listen. What changes is the quality of your preparation - which makes sessions more focused, higher signal, and easier for others to replicate if needed.

Live demo

Demo 1

Qualitative coding · live

Run the demo here. Walk through: 1. Show the research plan and transcripts 2. Show your pre-written codes 3. Show the AI generating the codebook collaboratively 4. Hand over transcripts for coding 5. Show synthesis phase using second brain notes Optional: Opus vs Sonnet comparison on one coding task.
"Here are my transcripts. What are the themes?"
This is the YOLO prompt. You get something back. It looks structured. It's often slop. The categories are obvious, the nuance is gone, and you can't trust it because you didn't guide it. Garbage in, garbage out - at the synthesis stage too.

My way to code

  • Read second brain on coding best practices.
  • Write 4-5 codes yourself first - give the AI a starting direction
  • "Here are my initial codes. Here's my research objective. Generate a full codebook."
  • Iterate until solid. Then hand over the transcripts.
"Here are my transcripts. Here's the codebook. Code these."
Step 1: Write 4-5 codes yourself before touching the AI. Force yourself to read one or two transcripts, form some initial impressions, name them. A good starting direction produces dramatically better results. Step 2: AI generates codebook collaboratively. You're reviewing, pushing back, refining - not just delegating. Step 3: Hand over the transcripts with the codebook. Like printing them out, highlighting, cutting into chunks, posting on a wall - except it takes minutes. 1 hour of interview = traditionally 1-2 hours of analysis. Now it's a coffee break. That's not a marginal improvement - it's a category change.

The codebook prompt

Research objective: how architects find and manage freelance work

My initial codes (seeds): move trigger · selection criteria · search process · space setup · help-seeking

Read from my second brain: "Open Coding", "Codebook"
Go through each transcript. Grow from 5 to 10-15 codes.

Three parts: the objective anchors the AI in what we're learning. The initial codes are the most important input - they come from a real first read, they show direction. The second brain pull is what makes the output reflect qualitative best practice rather than just pattern-matching on frequency.

The coding prompt

Finalized codebook is in results/codebook.md

Read from my second brain: "Closed Coding", "Thematic Analysis"
Code all 3 transcripts using only these codes.

Save as one file per code, named with occurrence count.
Flag anything that doesn't fit in a separate file.

Much simpler. All the complexity is upstream - in the codebook quality, the initial codes you wrote yourself. This prompt is short by design. The AI is executing a framework you designed, not making judgment calls. That's the distinction.

Walk away. Come back with fresh eyes.

Every major milestone in this process - codes in, codes out; transcripts in, coded data out - is a natural moment to step back. Don't immediately synthesize. Let it settle. Sleep on it. This is where human judgment matters most, and you can't rush it.

Now synthesize

  • You have coded data. Now what?
  • Feed your notes on what good synthesis looks like alongside the coded data
  • "I saw this" + "I know this" = insight
  • The AI handles the pattern. You bring the judgment.
  • MCP reconnects your knowledge
This is the second moment where your second brain becomes active. First it helped you plan - research objective, recruiting criteria. Now it helps you make sense of what you found. Feed the coded data alongside your notes on insight anatomy, what makes a finding vs. an observation, how to tell a research story. The output is calibrated by your craft knowledge, not just pattern-matched from the transcripts. The circle closes: second brain - project head start - research - coding - synthesis guided by second brain again.

The synthesis prompt

Read: coded data in results/coding/ + research plan

Read from my second brain: "Insight", "Customer research synthesis", "Storytelling"

Form 3-5 insights - not summaries, not findings.
For each: what's true · why it's non-obvious · evidence · design opportunity

The structure does a lot of work here. "Not summaries, not findings" is a real constraint - the AI will default to summaries if you let it. Naming the four fields forces it into insight anatomy: the claim, the challenge to an assumption, the evidence, and the open design space. Second brain notes on insight and synthesis shape what it considers a valid insight vs. a weak one.

Three wins from this workflow

I call it data accessibility
Transition to the democratization section. These are three real stories, three different contexts.

Win 1: The team can query the research

  • Markdown files in a shared repo
  • Anyone can point their AI tool at it and get up to speed
  • No license required. No gatekeeper. Researcher stops being the bottleneck.
No Dovetail license required. No gatekeeper. A developer, a PM, a new team member - they can ask questions, get context, understand what was found. The researcher stops being the bottleneck.

Win 2: The client ran the sessions

  • High-quality prep artifacts - non-researchers facilitated onboarding
  • Script with built-in "pause here, go deeper" prompts
  • Client's managers ran more than half the sessions themselves
Real story: I prepared a script, a brief, an accompanying presentation for onboarding sessions with an LLM-based product. I expected to run all 10 sessions. The client's managers volunteered. The script had built-in "pause here, go deeper" prompts. They handled more than half. They built empathy with their own direct reports. This wouldn't have worked with sloppy prep.

Win 3: Ditch the tool

  • PM/CEO colleague: tried Dovetail for a week, too steep a learning curve
  • Switched to plain text + LLM
  • Time saved went straight into strategy and pivots
The "proper" research tool was the obstacle. The simpler approach freed up time for higher-value conversations. This loops back to the slop warning - the reason this worked is that the plain text artifacts were good. Easily readable, well-structured, trustworthy.

You are the gatekeeper.

  • Slop is easy to generate. Easy to spot. Hard to undo.
  • It's a risk to your professional reputation.
  • Don't ship it if you don't like it.
Say this directly. "I have to say this because I've seen it. The tooling makes it trivially easy to produce mediocre output and ship it without thinking. That's a professional reputation risk. If you wouldn't put your name on it without the AI, don't put your name on it with it."

Part 2

Delivery

Second diamond. Shift from research/synthesis to prototyping and handover.

The context

  • 4+ months to find a new place with a partner and a dog.
  • I hate the noise of rental portals.
  • Custom watchdog capable of scanning:
    • dog-friendly listings
    • good light conditions
    • preferable area in town
The apartment search is miserable - spam, missed listings, WhatsApp link-ping-pong with my partner. And rental portals don't even let you filter for dogs - which eliminates a third to a half of listings for us. So I built a tool.

What I built

  • Scrapes listings from real estate portals
  • Scores on hard attributes (area, size, price) and soft ones (dog-friendly, ...)
  • Notifies both of us when a listing passes the threshold
  • Each of us ranks independently - hot or not
  • Match → book a viewing + visit management
No more sending links over WhatsApp. No more "did you see that one?" conversations. The tool does the filtering; we just respond to what passes. It's essentially Tinder for apartments - or agile retrospective voting, if you prefer that metaphor.

As easy as that

work on issue 122
That's it. The entire prompt. The spec is in the GitHub issue - context, requirements, constraints. Claude reads it, implements it, opens a PR. The brevity is the whole point. Compare this to the three prompts from the research side.

Prototyping workflow

  1. Write the spec in a GitHub issue
  2. Send Claude code to implement it
  3. Fine-tune layout with Paper.design
  4. PR created on GitHub, reviewed by Copilot
  5. Preview auto-deployed, tests run
  6. Auto deploy if it's good
The spec lives in a GitHub issue - plain text, no special format. Claude picks it up, implements it, opens a PR. Copilot reviews the PR. A preview URL is auto-deployed so you can click through it before merging. Tests run in CI. No Figma. No back-and-forth with a developer. Source of truth stays in code.

The infra looks heavier than it is

  • PR auto-previews
  • Git workflow
  • MCP server connections
    • One-time setup - 1-2 hours for someone technical
I know it can look intimidating from the outside. But the heavy lifting is done once, and usually by someone who already knows how. After that, it just works. Don't let the initial complexity stop you from asking your favourite engineer for an afternoon.

GitHub SpecKit

  • Learn a mental model of GitHub - it's not hard
  • Ask your favourite engineer for 30 minutes - they'll probably love explaining it
  • Discovery → findings in markdown → SpecKit → engineering handoff
  • SpecKit is not a silver bullet - even plain "plan mode" in Claude could help a lot
Figma is great. But when the source of truth lives in a design tool, it diverges from what's actually built. Text and code don't diverge in the same way - they are what's running. SpecKit is GitHub's tool for specifying features and products. After research synthesis, I use it to formalise what we learned into specs, then hand off to engineering with a shared reference point. The conversation between design and engineering becomes less "here's a Figma link" and more "here's what we know, here's what we're building, here's why."

Closing

To wrap this up

Take a breath

1. You don't need to become technical

The barrier is lower than it looks.

A short conversation with a developer you trust is enough to get started.

"I'm not a technical person" can become a self-limiting story. The tools today are genuinely more accessible than they were even a year ago, and they keep meeting you further where you are. You don't need to learn to code. You just need to ask one honest question. The rest follows.

Iterate

2. Experiment with your process

What worked 6 months ago is already different.

The tool landscape moves fast. Center around one major tool, connect things to it incrementally. Don't try to rebuild your whole workflow at once. Notice what's slow, what's frustrating, and see if there's a better path.

Start now

3. Jump on to proven parts soon

The later you wait, the harder it gets.

No need to be an early adopter of 'that new thing' - but don't wait until it's too late.

Not because you'll be left behind - though that's real - but because the learning compounds. Every week of practice with this stuff is a week of intuition built. Start now, even imperfectly.
